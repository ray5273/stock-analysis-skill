# Uncomfortable Question Rubric

Use this reference to keep `Uncomfortable Questions` specific to the company type instead of falling back to generic risk bullets.

## How To Use It

1. Pick the closest business archetype before drafting the full memo.
2. Start with the archetype's default question set.
3. Rewrite each question so it points at the actual company, disclosure gaps, and decision frame in the active memo.
4. Keep only the 3-6 questions most likely to change the stance.

## Archetype: Captive IT Services / SI / Digital Transformation

Use this when the company still depends heavily on affiliated customers, large transformation projects, managed services, or internal-group spending.

- How much of the reported growth is truly non-captive versus simply a different reporting bucket for group work?
- If the top captive customers slow capex or IT spending, what breaks first in revenue, margin, or utilization?
- Are cloud, AI, or data-center wins recurring economics, or one-time build revenue dressed up as platform growth?
- Is the market giving a pure-software or AI-infrastructure multiple to a business that still behaves like project SI?
- Do the filings actually prove margin expansion durability, or just one good project mix window?

## Archetype: Defense Prime / Defense Electronics

Use this when the company relies on backlog, weapon systems, export programs, or government procurement cycles.

- Does backlog convert into cash and margin on the timeline implied by the stock, or just sit on paper?
- How concentrated is the program base, the customer base, or the export-country mix once you get past headline backlog?
- Which part of the story is already proven production, and which part is still option value from MOU, preferred-bid, or platform hopes?
- If procurement timing slips by a year, what happens to the earnings profile that the market is paying for now?
- Is the current valuation assuming export scale before the filings show export mix changing structurally?

## Archetype: Holding Company / Conglomerate Discount Story

Use this when the listed vehicle is mainly an ownership wrapper, capital allocator, or governance rerating story.

- What exactly closes the discount from here, real cash returns, simplified structure, or just narrative?
- Are minority holders meaningfully protected, or is the discount deserved because control still dominates economics?
- How much of NAV is liquid, distributable, or monetizable without optimistic assumptions?
- Is the rerating case based on one-off actions, or a repeatable capital-allocation regime?
- If the market stops hoping for governance change, what business or cash floor supports the stock?

## Archetype: Order-Driven Plant / Infrastructure / EPC

Use this when the company depends on large contracts, backlog, milestone billing, or execution-heavy delivery cycles.

- Does reported order intake say anything about future margin, or only about top-line visibility?
- How much of backlog is cancellable, low margin, front-loaded in working capital, or exposed to customer financing?
- Are investors mistaking revenue recognition timing for true demand improvement?
- If execution slips or cost overruns hit one large project, how much of the equity story breaks?
- Are there related-party or customer concentration dynamics hidden inside the project mix?

## Archetype: Cyclical Manufacturing / Materials / Commodity-Linked Supplier

Use this when the company depends on utilization, spreads, inventory cycles, customer concentration, or commodity swings.

- How much of the recovery is real end-demand versus inventory refill or mix timing?
- If utilization or spreads fall back to mid-cycle, what happens to earnings power?
- Are investors paying a structural multiple for what is still mostly cyclical improvement?
- How exposed is the company to one customer, one chemistry, or one end market despite the diversification story?
- Which cash-flow or balance-sheet line proves this is more resilient than the last cycle?

## Archetype: Recurring Software / Platform / Data Business

Use this when the story depends on recurring revenue, product adoption, pricing power, or low-churn claims.

- Is the recurring revenue truly contractual and sticky, or held up by service work and bespoke implementation?
- Are new products driving durable upsell, or just early adoption that may not monetize cleanly?
- What does the filing say about churn, retention, or customer concentration, and what is merely implied?
- Is the market paying for software economics before the gross-margin and cash-conversion profile show up?
- Which customer behavior would prove the product is mission-critical rather than a nice-to-have budget line?
